# Meteorology — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

Maritime meteorology is a critical knowledge area for the OOW Unlimited examination. An officer must understand weather systems, their formation and movement, the interpretation of weather charts and forecasts, and the practical implications of weather on ship safety and navigation. The ability to anticipate deteriorating weather and take appropriate action is a core competency. The MCA examiner will expect candidates to demonstrate not just theoretical knowledge of atmospheric science, but the practical ability to use meteorological information for voyage planning and safe navigation.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **SOLAS Regulation V/5** — Meteorological services and warnings
- **SOLAS Regulation V/31** — Danger messages (obligation to report dangerous weather)
- **WMO (World Meteorological Organization)** — International meteorological codes and standards
- **The Mariner's Handbook (NP100)** — Chapter 6 (Meteorology)
- **Admiralty List of Radio Signals (ALRS) Volume 4** — Meteorological observation stations
- **IMO Resolution A.528(13)** — Recommendations on weather routing
- **Beaufort Wind Scale** — Standard maritime wind force scale

## Core Concepts

### Atmospheric Pressure and Pressure Systems

- **Atmospheric pressure**: Measured in hectopascals (hPa) or millibars (mb) — they are equivalent. Standard sea level pressure is 1013.25 hPa.
- **High pressure systems (anticyclones)**: Clockwise circulation in the Northern Hemisphere (anticlockwise in the Southern Hemisphere). Associated with settled weather, light winds, and good visibility — though fog may form in moist conditions.
- **Low pressure systems (depressions/cyclones)**: Anticlockwise circulation in the Northern Hemisphere. Associated with frontal systems, strong winds, cloud, rain, and reduced visibility. Wind strength is proportional to pressure gradient — the closer the isobars, the stronger the wind.
- **Buys Ballot's Law**: If you stand with your back to the wind in the Northern Hemisphere, low pressure is to your left. Essential for locating the centre of a depression.

### Frontal Systems

- **Warm front**: Warm air mass advancing and rising over cold air. Cloud sequence: Ci, Cs, As, Ns (high to low). Steady rain, falling pressure, backing wind (in NH), visibility deteriorating. Typically a gentle slope (1:150).
- **Cold front**: Cold air mass advancing and undercutting warm air. Steeper slope (1:50). Sudden drop in temperature, veering wind, clearance after passage, heavy showers or thunderstorms, pressure rises sharply.
- **Occluded front**: When a cold front catches up with a warm front. Cold occlusion (cold front undercuts warm front air mass and warm front air mass) or warm occlusion (cold front rides over colder air ahead of warm front).
- **Warm sector**: The area of warm air between the warm front and cold front. Typically overcast with low stratus, drizzle, poor visibility.

### Tropical Revolving Storms (TRS)

- Known as hurricanes (Atlantic/East Pacific), typhoons (West Pacific), cyclones (Indian Ocean/South Pacific).
- **Formation requirements**: Sea surface temperature above 26.5C, latitude at least 5 degrees from equator (Coriolis effect needed), low wind shear, existing disturbance.
- **Structure**: Eye (calm, clear), eyewall (most intense winds), spiral rain bands.
- **Dangerous and navigable semicircles**: In the Northern Hemisphere, the right-hand semicircle (relative to the storm's track) is the dangerous semicircle — winds drive vessels into the storm's path. The left-hand semicircle is the navigable semicircle.
- **Avoidance rules (Northern Hemisphere)**:
  - In the dangerous semicircle: Bring the wind on the starboard bow and make best speed.
  - In the navigable semicircle: Bring the wind on the starboard quarter and make best speed.
  - Ahead of the storm's path: Bring the wind on the starboard quarter and make best speed to cross the track ahead.
- **Warning signs**: Long swell from unusual direction, falling barometer (especially rapid fall), cirrus clouds radiating from a point on the horizon, oppressive heat and humidity.

### Sea and Swell

- **Wind waves (sea)**: Generated by local wind. Height depends on wind speed, duration, and fetch.
- **Swell**: Waves that have travelled beyond their area of generation. Longer wavelength, more regular.
- **Significant wave height (Hs)**: The average height of the highest one-third of waves. This is what is reported in forecasts.
- **Rogue waves**: Abnormally large waves (can exceed 2x Hs). More likely in areas where waves oppose current (e.g., Agulhas Current).

### Fog

- **Advection fog**: Warm, moist air moving over a cold sea surface. Common in areas where warm and cold currents meet (e.g., Grand Banks). Can persist for days and cover vast areas.
- **Radiation fog**: Formed over land at night by radiative cooling, can drift out to sea in the morning. Usually thin and burns off.
- **Sea fog**: A form of advection fog. The OOW must reduce speed, post additional lookouts, sound fog signals, and use radar.

## Common Exam Questions

1. Describe the weather sequence you would expect during the passage of a depression across your vessel.
2. How would you determine whether you are in the dangerous or navigable semicircle of a TRS?
3. What avoidance action would you take if you suspected a TRS approaching in the Northern Hemisphere?
4. Explain the formation and characteristics of advection fog.
5. How do you use a barograph to predict weather changes?
6. What is the significance of a rapidly falling barometer at sea?
7. What are your obligations under SOLAS V/31 regarding weather observations and reports?

## Key Points to Remember

- **Buys Ballot's Law** is your most practical tool for quickly assessing the location of a low pressure centre relative to your vessel.
- **A barometric pressure drop of more than 5 hPa in 3 hours** indicates severe weather is approaching and the Master should be called immediately.
- **TRS avoidance**: The 34-knot wind radius can extend hundreds of miles from the eye. Early avoidance saves lives — do not wait until the storm is close.
- **Fog**: Reduce speed to a safe speed under Rule 6 of COLREGS. Sound fog signals under Rule 35. Post lookouts. Use radar. Plot other vessels. Do NOT simply rely on AIS.
- **Weather routing services**: Available commercially and can save significant fuel and reduce heavy weather exposure. The OOW should be able to discuss their use.
- **Backing and veering**: In the Northern Hemisphere, wind backs (shifts anticlockwise) ahead of a warm front and veers (shifts clockwise) behind a cold front.

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners want to see that the candidate can interpret weather information practically, not just recite theory. They will often present a scenario — "you notice the barometer falling rapidly and wind increasing from the southeast" — and expect the candidate to identify the likely weather system, determine their position relative to it, and articulate appropriate action. TRS avoidance is a perennial favourite. Candidates should also be able to read and interpret a synoptic weather chart, identifying fronts, pressure centres, and expected weather in a given area. Knowledge of fog types and the appropriate actions under COLREGS is frequently tested alongside meteorology.

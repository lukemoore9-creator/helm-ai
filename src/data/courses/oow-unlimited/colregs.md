# COLREGS (International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea) — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea 1972 (COLREGs) form the cornerstone of safe navigation and are arguably the most heavily examined topic in the MCA OOW Unlimited oral examination. These regulations, adopted by the IMO under Convention on the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea, apply to all vessels upon the high seas and in all waters connected therewith navigable by seagoing vessels. An OOW candidate must demonstrate not just rote knowledge of the rules, but the ability to apply them in complex, real-world scenarios involving multiple vessels, restricted visibility, and narrow channels.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **COLREGs 1972** as amended (IMO)
- **Part A — General** (Rules 1-3): Application, responsibility, general definitions
- **Part B — Steering and Sailing Rules**
  - Section I (Rules 4-10): Conduct in any condition of visibility
  - Section II (Rules 11-18): Conduct of vessels in sight of one another
  - Section III (Rule 19): Conduct in restricted visibility
- **Part C — Lights and Shapes** (Rules 20-31)
- **Part D — Sound and Light Signals** (Rules 32-37)
- **Part E — Exemptions** (Rule 38)
- **Annex I**: Positioning and technical details of lights and shapes
- **Annex II**: Additional signals for fishing vessels in close proximity
- **Annex III**: Technical details of sound signal appliances
- **Annex IV**: Distress signals

## Core Concepts

### Rule 2 — Responsibility
Often overlooked but critically important. Rule 2(a) states that nothing in the rules exonerates any vessel from the consequences of neglect of any precaution required by the ordinary practice of seamen, or the special circumstances of the case. Rule 2(b) allows departure from the rules to avoid immediate danger. Examiners frequently test whether candidates understand that blind compliance with rules does not absolve responsibility.

### Rule 5 — Lookout
Every vessel shall at all times maintain a proper lookout by sight and hearing as well as by all available means appropriate in the prevailing circumstances and conditions so as to make a full appraisal of the situation and of the risk of collision. This means using radar, AIS, VHF, and any other available equipment — not just visual observation.

### Rule 6 — Safe Speed
Every vessel shall at all times proceed at a safe speed. Factors to consider include visibility, traffic density, manoeuvrability of the vessel, background light at night, state of wind/sea/current, draught in relation to available water depth, and radar limitations.

### Rule 7 — Risk of Collision
Risk of collision shall be deemed to exist if the compass bearing of an approaching vessel does not appreciably change. Candidates must understand that risk may sometimes exist even when an appreciable bearing change is evident, particularly with large vessels or tows at close range.

### Rule 8 — Action to Avoid Collision
Any alteration of course and/or speed shall be large enough to be readily apparent to another vessel observing visually or by radar. A succession of small alterations should be avoided. Action shall result in passing at a safe distance and shall be checked until the other vessel is finally past and clear.

### Rules 13-17 — Overtaking, Head-on, Crossing, and Stand-on/Give-way
- **Rule 13**: An overtaking vessel shall keep clear regardless of the relative bearing. A vessel is deemed overtaking when coming up with another from a direction more than 22.5 degrees abaft her beam.
- **Rule 14**: Head-on situation — both vessels alter course to starboard.
- **Rule 15**: Crossing situation — the vessel which has the other on her starboard side shall keep out of the way.
- **Rule 17**: Action by stand-on vessel — shall keep her course and speed but may take action when it becomes apparent the give-way vessel is not taking appropriate action.

### Rule 19 — Restricted Visibility
Applies to vessels not in sight of one another navigating in or near an area of restricted visibility. No give-way/stand-on relationship exists. Every vessel must proceed at a safe speed, and any vessel detecting another by radar alone shall determine if a close-quarters situation is developing and take avoiding action in ample time. If action consists of an alteration of course: avoid altering to port for a vessel forward of the beam (except overtaking), and avoid altering towards a vessel abeam or abaft the beam.

## Common Exam Questions

1. Describe the actions you would take upon detecting a vessel on radar at 12 miles in restricted visibility with a constant bearing and decreasing range.
2. Two power-driven vessels are crossing. The give-way vessel takes no action. At what point does the stand-on vessel take action, and what action is appropriate?
3. You are proceeding down a narrow channel. A vessel is crossing the channel ahead of you. Who gives way and under which rule?
4. Explain the difference between Rule 18 (responsibilities between vessels) and the hierarchy it establishes.
5. A sailing vessel is overtaking a power-driven vessel. Who gives way and why?
6. What lights would a vessel constrained by her draught display? At what point can a vessel claim to be constrained by her draught?

## Key Points to Remember

- **Overtaking always gives way** — Rule 13 overrides Rules 14-15. An overtaking sailing vessel gives way to a power-driven vessel being overtaken.
- **Rule 18 hierarchy**: NUC > RAM > CBD > vessel engaged in fishing > sailing vessel > power-driven vessel. But this does NOT apply in narrow channels (Rule 9), TSS (Rule 10), or overtaking (Rule 13).
- **Restricted visibility (Rule 19)**: There is NO stand-on vessel. Both vessels must take action.
- **Narrow channels (Rule 9)**: Vessels less than 20m, sailing vessels, and fishing vessels shall not impede a vessel that can safely navigate only within a narrow channel.
- **TSS (Rule 10)**: Vessels less than 20m, sailing vessels, and fishing vessels shall not impede a power-driven vessel following a traffic lane.
- **"Shall not impede" vs "Give way"**: A vessel required not to impede another remains obliged to comply with Rules 13-17 if risk of collision exists.

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners will present complex multi-vessel scenarios and expect candidates to identify applicable rules, articulate the reasoning behind their actions, and demonstrate they understand the hierarchy and exceptions. They particularly focus on Rule 2 (responsibility and good seamanship beyond the rules), Rule 19 (candidates often incorrectly apply give-way logic in fog), and the interplay between Rules 9, 10, and 18. Candidates who can explain why the rules require certain actions — not just what the rules say — will perform strongly.

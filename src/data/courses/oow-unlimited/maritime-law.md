# Maritime Law — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

Maritime law provides the legal framework within which the shipping industry operates. For an OOW Unlimited candidate, a working knowledge of the key conventions, the role of flag states and port states, the legal responsibilities of the Master and officers, and the principles governing collision liability, salvage, and pollution are essential. The MCA examiner will not expect the depth of knowledge required of a solicitor, but will expect the candidate to understand their legal obligations, the consequences of non-compliance, and the relationship between international conventions and domestic law as applied to UK-flagged and foreign-flagged vessels in UK waters.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **UNCLOS 1982** — United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea
- **SOLAS 1974** as amended — Safety of Life at Sea
- **MARPOL 73/78** — Prevention of Pollution from Ships
- **STCW 1978** as amended — Standards of Training, Certification and Watchkeeping
- **COLREGs 1972** — Prevention of Collisions at Sea
- **International Load Line Convention 1966** — Protocol of 1988
- **CLC 1992** — Civil Liability Convention (oil pollution damage)
- **Bunkers Convention 2001** — Civil liability for bunker oil pollution damage
- **Nairobi Wreck Removal Convention 2007**
- **Hague-Visby Rules** — Carriage of goods by sea
- **MLC 2006** — Maritime Labour Convention
- **Merchant Shipping Act 1995** (UK domestic legislation)
- **Paris MOU** — Port State Control agreement

## Core Concepts

### Zones of the Sea (UNCLOS)

UNCLOS establishes the legal framework for maritime zones:

- **Internal waters**: Waters landward of the baseline. Full sovereignty of the coastal state. Foreign vessels have no right of passage (unless in ports, which have customary access).
- **Territorial sea**: Up to 12 nautical miles from the baseline. Sovereignty of the coastal state, subject to the right of innocent passage for foreign vessels.
- **Contiguous zone**: Up to 24 nautical miles from the baseline. The coastal state may exercise control to prevent infringement of customs, fiscal, immigration, or sanitary laws.
- **Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ)**: Up to 200 nautical miles from the baseline. The coastal state has sovereign rights over natural resources and jurisdiction over marine scientific research and environmental protection. Freedom of navigation and overflight for all states.
- **High seas**: Beyond the EEZ. Freedom of navigation, fishing, scientific research, and laying submarine cables. No state may claim sovereignty. Vessels are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the flag state.
- **Continental shelf**: Extends beyond the territorial sea to the outer edge of the continental margin, or 200 nm from the baseline, whichever is greater.

### Flag State and Port State Responsibilities

**Flag state obligations:**
- Register vessels and issue certificates (safety, manning, pollution prevention).
- Ensure vessels comply with international conventions.
- Conduct surveys and inspections.
- Investigate casualties involving flagged vessels.
- The flag state is responsible for the condition and compliance of its fleet.

**Port State Control (PSC):**
- Port states have the right to inspect foreign-flagged vessels visiting their ports (SOLAS Regulation I/19, MARPOL, STCW, MLC).
- **Paris MOU**: The European and North Atlantic PSC regime. Inspections are risk-based using a ship risk profile (type, age, flag, recognised organisation, company, inspection history).
- **Inspection scope**: Verify certificates, check crew competence, inspect safety equipment, review ISM/ISPS compliance, examine living and working conditions (MLC).
- **Detention**: If deficiencies are found that are clearly hazardous to safety, health, or the environment, the vessel may be detained until the deficiency is rectified. Detention is published and affects the flag state and company profiles.
- **No more favourable treatment (NMFT)**: Vessels flagged in non-party states must not receive more favourable treatment than vessels of party states.

### Master's Authority and Responsibilities

- **Overriding authority**: SOLAS V/34.1 — The shipowner, charterer, or any other person shall not prevent or restrict the Master from taking any decision which is necessary for the safety of life at sea and the protection of the marine environment.
- **ISM Code**: The Master has the overriding authority and responsibility to make decisions regarding safety and pollution prevention, and to request the company's assistance as necessary.
- **Criminal liability**: The Master and officers can be held personally criminally liable for pollution offences, safety violations, and negligence. Under UK law (Merchant Shipping Act 1995), offences include operating an unseaworthy ship, failing to render assistance to persons in distress, and breaching MARPOL discharge requirements.
- **Obligation to render assistance**: SOLAS V/33 and customary maritime law require the Master to render assistance to any person in distress at sea, so far as they can do so without serious danger to the ship, crew, and passengers.

### Collision Liability

- **Convention on Certain Rules Relating to Collisions 1910 (Brussels Convention)**: Establishes the principle of proportional liability. If both vessels are at fault, liability is apportioned according to the degree of fault.
- **Duty after collision**: Under maritime law and the Merchant Shipping Act, a vessel involved in a collision must stand by the other vessel, render assistance, exchange particulars (vessel name, port of registry, last port and next port), and report the collision to the relevant authorities.
- **Collision investigation**: Marine Accident Investigation Branch (MAIB) in the UK. The purpose is to establish causes and improve safety, not to apportion blame (though the report may be used in legal proceedings).

### Salvage

- **International Convention on Salvage 1989**: Any person who renders assistance to a vessel or property in danger at sea is entitled to a salvage award. The award is based on: the value of the property salved, the skill and effort of the salvors, the degree of danger to the salved vessel and the salving vessel, and the time and expense incurred.
- **LOF (Lloyd's Open Form)**: The standard salvage contract. "No cure, no pay" — if the salvage is unsuccessful, the salvor receives no payment (with exceptions for environmental salvage under Article 14 — special compensation).
- **Master's authority to accept salvage**: The Master has the authority to enter into a salvage agreement on behalf of the shipowner. This is a significant legal power and responsibility.

### Pollution Liability

- **CLC 1992**: Strict liability on the registered shipowner for pollution damage from persistent oil cargo. Shipowner must maintain insurance (P&I club). Liability is limited unless caused by personal act or omission with intent or recklessly.
- **Bunkers Convention 2001**: Strict liability on the registered shipowner for pollution from bunker fuel. Insurance mandatory.
- **UK law**: The Merchant Shipping Act 1995 implements these conventions. Criminal penalties for deliberate or negligent discharge.
- **Reporting obligations**: MARPOL Protocol I — any discharge or probable discharge must be reported to the nearest coastal state without delay.

### Maritime Labour Convention (MLC) 2006

- Often called the "Seafarers' Bill of Rights."
- Covers: minimum age, medical fitness, training and qualifications, conditions of employment (seafarers' employment agreement), hours of work and rest, entitlement to leave, repatriation, accommodation and food, health and safety, medical care, welfare, social security, and complaint procedures.
- Ships must carry a Maritime Labour Certificate (MLC Certificate) and a Declaration of Maritime Labour Compliance (DMLC).
- Enforceable through PSC inspections.

## Common Exam Questions

1. What are your obligations as OOW if your vessel is involved in a collision?
2. Explain the role and powers of Port State Control.
3. Under what circumstances can a port state detain a vessel?
4. What is the Master's overriding authority under SOLAS and the ISM Code?
5. Describe the maritime zones under UNCLOS and what rights the coastal state has in each.
6. What is LOF and how does salvage law work?
7. What are your personal legal liabilities as a deck officer regarding pollution?
8. What does the Maritime Labour Convention cover?

## Key Points to Remember

- **The Master's overriding authority is sacrosanct.** No charterer or owner can override the Master's professional judgement on safety matters. If the OOW believes safety is compromised, they must inform the Master, and the Master must act.
- **Personal criminal liability**: Officers can be prosecuted personally for pollution offences and safety failures. "I was following orders" is not a defence if the order was unlawful.
- **After a collision**: Stand by, render assistance, exchange particulars, make no admission of liability, record all facts and evidence (photos, logbook entries, weather conditions, radar recordings, VDR data).
- **PSC detention is public record** — it affects the vessel, the company, the flag state, and the classification society. Preventing detention through proper maintenance and compliance is part of the OOW's daily responsibility.
- **12 nm territorial sea, 200 nm EEZ** — these fundamental UNCLOS distances must be known.
- **Salvage**: The Master can sign LOF without the owner's prior approval. Time is critical in salvage situations.

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners test maritime law at a practical rather than academic level. They want to know that the candidate understands their legal obligations in common situations: collision, pollution, rendering assistance, PSC inspection, and interaction with port authorities. Candidates should be able to explain the flag state vs port state relationship, the meaning and implications of detention, and the reporting obligations under various conventions. The examiner may present scenarios (oil spill, collision at sea, PSC inspector boarding) and expect the candidate to articulate the correct procedures and legal requirements. Understanding of the Master's authority and the OOW's personal liability is particularly important.

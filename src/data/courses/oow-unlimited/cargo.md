# Cargo Operations — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

Cargo operations encompass the planning, loading, stowage, securing, monitoring, and discharge of cargo aboard merchant vessels. For an OOW Unlimited candidate, this is a broad and practical topic covering general cargo, containers, bulk cargoes, and dangerous goods. The MCA examiner will expect candidates to demonstrate understanding of cargo care principles, the regulatory framework governing cargo carriage, the hazards associated with different cargo types, and the OOW's responsibilities during cargo operations. Safe and efficient cargo handling is central to commercial shipping and directly impacts the vessel's stability, structural integrity, and the safety of the crew.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **SOLAS Chapter VI** — Carriage of Cargoes and Oil Fuels
- **SOLAS Chapter VII** — Carriage of Dangerous Goods
- **IMSBC Code** (International Maritime Solid Bulk Cargoes Code) — mandatory under SOLAS VI
- **IMDG Code** (International Maritime Dangerous Goods Code) — mandatory under SOLAS VII
- **CSS Code** (Code of Safe Practice for Cargo Stowage and Securing) — IMO Resolution A.714(17) as amended
- **International Grain Code** — mandatory under SOLAS VI/9
- **CTU Code** (Code of Practice for Packing of Cargo Transport Units) — IMO/ILO/UNECE
- **Hague-Visby Rules** — International convention on carriage of goods by sea (bills of lading)
- **MCA MSN 1855** — Cargo securing arrangements

## Core Concepts

### General Principles of Cargo Care

The fundamental duty is to carry cargo safely from port of loading to port of discharge without damage to the cargo, the vessel, or persons. Key principles:

- **Protection from water damage**: Hatch covers must be weathertight (tested by hose test or ultrasonic). Bilge systems must be operational. Ventilation must be managed to prevent condensation (ship sweat vs cargo sweat).
- **Ventilation**: The rule of three: ventilate when the dew point of the outside air is lower than the dew point of the air in the hold. Use the dew point rule to decide whether to ventilate. Hygroscopic cargoes (grain, timber, cotton) are particularly susceptible to moisture damage.
- **Separation and segregation**: Different cargoes must be separated to prevent contamination, chemical reaction, or taint. The IMDG Code provides detailed segregation tables for dangerous goods.
- **Securing**: Cargo must be secured to prevent shifting in a seaway. The CSS Code provides guidance. Lashing calculations consider the vessel's expected accelerations based on the GM, vessel dimensions, and stowage position.

### Bulk Cargoes (IMSBC Code)

The IMSBC Code classifies bulk cargoes into three groups:

- **Group A — Cargoes that may liquefy**: These include iron ore fines, nickel ore, bauxite, and some concentrates. The cargo has a moisture content that, if exceeded, can cause the cargo to liquefy and shift. The **Transportable Moisture Limit (TML)** is the maximum moisture content at which a cargo can be safely transported. The actual moisture content must be below the TML, and a certificate must be provided by the shipper. The **Flow Moisture Point (FMP)** is the moisture content at which flow state occurs — TML is 90% of FMP. The "can test" is a simple check: fill a can half full, strike it on a hard surface — if moisture or flow appears, do NOT load.
- **Group B — Cargoes that possess a chemical hazard**: Includes materials that can be toxic, corrosive, flammable, or cause oxygen depletion (e.g., coal, direct reduced iron, seed cake).
- **Group C — Cargoes that are neither liable to liquefy nor possess chemical hazards**: Most general bulk cargoes.

**Coal**: A Group B cargo with specific hazards — methane emission (explosion risk), self-heating (spontaneous combustion), oxygen depletion, and corrosion. Requires monitoring of cargo hold atmosphere (methane, CO, CO2, O2). Ventilation requirements depend on whether the coal is prone to self-heating — surface ventilation only (do not ventilate through the body of the cargo).

### Containers

- **Container weights**: Verified Gross Mass (VGM) is mandatory under SOLAS VI/2 (amendment effective 1 July 2016). The shipper must provide the VGM before loading. Two methods: Method 1 (weigh the packed container) or Method 2 (weigh all contents and add the tare weight).
- **Container securing**: Bay plans, lashing patterns, stack weights, and tier limits. The CSS Code Annex 13 and vessel-specific Cargo Securing Manual provide the requirements.
- **Container inspections**: External condition (CSC plate validity — maximum 5 years from manufacture, then 30-month or ACEP inspection), door seals, structural integrity.
- **Reefer containers**: Temperature monitoring, pre-trip inspection, power supply requirements, air circulation.

### Dangerous Goods (IMDG Code)

The IMDG Code classifies dangerous goods into 9 classes:

1. **Class 1**: Explosives (divisions 1.1-1.6)
2. **Class 2**: Gases (2.1 flammable, 2.2 non-flammable/non-toxic, 2.3 toxic)
3. **Class 3**: Flammable liquids
4. **Class 4**: Flammable solids (4.1), spontaneously combustible (4.2), dangerous when wet (4.3)
5. **Class 5**: Oxidising substances (5.1), organic peroxides (5.2)
6. **Class 6**: Toxic substances (6.1), infectious substances (6.2)
7. **Class 7**: Radioactive material
8. **Class 8**: Corrosive substances
9. **Class 9**: Miscellaneous dangerous substances

**Documentation**: Dangerous Goods Manifest, Dangerous Goods Declaration, container/vehicle packing certificate. The DG manifest must include: proper shipping name, UN number, class, packing group, stowage location, and quantity.

**Segregation**: The IMDG Code segregation table specifies four levels — "away from," "separated from," "separated by a complete compartment or hold from," and "separated longitudinally by an intervening complete compartment or hold from."

**EmS (Emergency Schedules)**: Fire schedule and spillage schedule for each substance. The OOW must know where to find these and how to use them.

### Cargo Documentation

- **Cargo plan**: Stowage plan showing location of all cargo.
- **Dangerous goods manifest**: Required to be available to shore-side emergency responders.
- **Stability calculations**: Loading computer or manual calculations for each cargo condition.
- **Mate's receipt / Bills of lading**: The OOW may be required to clause a mate's receipt if cargo is damaged or not as described.

## Common Exam Questions

1. What precautions would you take when loading a Group A bulk cargo under the IMSBC Code?
2. Explain the significance of the Transportable Moisture Limit and how you would verify it.
3. How would you manage the ventilation of a cargo of grain on a passage from the tropics to a cold climate?
4. What are the segregation requirements for IMDG Class 3 and Class 5.1 cargoes?
5. You notice water pooling on the surface of a bulk cargo in the hold. What are the implications and what action would you take?
6. Describe the VGM requirements for containers under SOLAS VI/2.
7. What hazards are associated with carrying coal in bulk?

## Key Points to Remember

- **TML and moisture content**: Never load a Group A cargo if the moisture content exceeds the TML. Request the shipper's certificate and verify independently (can test).
- **Ventilation decisions**: Always based on dew point comparison, not temperature alone.
- **Container VGM**: No VGM, no loading. This is a SOLAS requirement with no exceptions.
- **IMDG segregation**: Learn the segregation table format. "Separated from" means in a different compartment (hold or on deck separated by one container space). "Away from" means not in the same compartment or adjacent on deck.
- **Cargo securing**: The vessel's Cargo Securing Manual is approved and must be followed. Lashing arrangements must account for the worst-case accelerations for the intended voyage.
- **Coal hazards**: Monitor atmosphere continuously. If methane exceeds 20% of the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL), seal the hold and stop ventilation. If CO increases, it may indicate self-heating — do not ventilate through the cargo.

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners will test practical knowledge of cargo operations relevant to the vessels the candidate has served on, but will also probe general cargo principles. Bulk cargo hazards (particularly liquefaction) are a frequent topic following several high-profile casualties. Candidates must demonstrate they understand the TML concept and can describe protective measures. Dangerous goods segregation is another common area — examiners expect candidates to be able to use the IMDG Code segregation table. The OOW's role during cargo operations (monitoring, recording, liaising with the terminal) is also examined, as is the candidate's understanding of how cargo affects the vessel's stability and stress.

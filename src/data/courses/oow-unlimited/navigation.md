# Navigation — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

Navigation is the practical heart of the OOW Unlimited examination. Candidates must demonstrate competence in passage planning, chart work, position fixing, tidal calculations, and the use of both traditional and electronic navigational methods. The MCA examiner expects an OOW to be capable of planning and executing a coastal or ocean passage safely, using all available resources, and to understand the limitations and errors inherent in every navigational method. This topic draws on SOLAS Chapter V and the associated IMO Resolutions governing navigational practices.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **SOLAS Chapter V** — Safety of Navigation
- **SOLAS Regulation V/34** — Safe Navigation and avoidance of dangerous situations
- **IMO Resolution A.893(21)** — Guidelines for Voyage Planning
- **IMO Resolution A.572(14)** — General Provisions on Ships' Routeing
- **IMO Resolution MSC.232(82)** — Revised Performance Standards for ECDIS
- **MGN 379** (MCA) — Voyage Planning
- **UKHO Admiralty Notices to Mariners** (NMs) and Annual Summary (NP247)
- **IHO Standards** for Electronic Navigational Charts (ENCs)

## Core Concepts

### Passage Planning (Four Stages)

As per IMO Resolution A.893(21), passage planning is divided into four stages:

1. **Appraisal**: Gathering all relevant information — charts (appropriate scale), publications (Sailing Directions, List of Lights, Tide Tables, Current Atlases), weather routing, port information, draught considerations, reporting requirements, and any navigational warnings (NAVAREA, coastal, local).

2. **Planning**: Plotting the intended track on appropriate charts, marking waypoints, identifying no-go areas, establishing clearing bearings and clearing ranges, calculating under-keel clearance (UKC), noting wheel-over positions for course alterations, identifying contingency anchorages, and determining abort points.

3. **Execution**: Monitoring the vessel's progress along the planned track, maintaining a proper lookout, fixing position at appropriate intervals (more frequently in coastal waters), comparing GPS positions with independent fixes, and recording all navigational events in the deck log.

4. **Monitoring**: Continuous cross-checking of position, speed, ETA, and tidal predictions against the plan. The OOW must be prepared to deviate from the plan if circumstances require.

### Position Fixing Methods

- **Visual bearings**: Using compass bearings of charted objects. Three-point fix preferred (cocked hat). Bearings should be taken in order of the most slowly changing bearing first.
- **Radar ranges and bearings**: Radar range is generally more accurate than radar bearing. A combination of two radar ranges gives a reliable fix. Parallel indexing is essential for monitoring the vessel's track.
- **GPS/GNSS**: Primary electronic fix. Candidates must understand HDOP, position accuracy, datum considerations (WGS 84), and the critical importance of cross-checking GPS with independent methods.
- **Celestial navigation**: Sun run and meridian altitude, star sights. While ECDIS has reduced reliance, the MCA still expects OOW candidates to understand the principles and be able to obtain a position line from a celestial observation.

### Chart Work and Corrections

- Paper charts must be corrected to the latest NM. Candidates should understand the difference between permanent, temporary, and preliminary NMs.
- ECDIS: ENCs are maintained via official update services. The OOW must understand ECDIS alarms, display settings, safety contour vs safety depth, and the limitations of ECDIS.
- The difference between a chart datum (vertical reference for depths) and a geodetic datum (horizontal reference for positions) must be understood.

### Tides and Tidal Streams

- Use of Admiralty Tide Tables (ATT) Volumes 1-3: Standard and secondary ports, time and height differences, tidal curves for interpolation.
- Calculation of under-keel clearance: Charted depth + height of tide - vessel draught = UKC. Squat and heel must also be factored in.
- Tidal stream atlases and tidal diamonds on charts for estimating set and drift.
- Spring and neap tidal ranges, and interpolation between them.

### Compass Errors

- **Variation**: The angle between true north and magnetic north, found on the compass rose of the chart. It changes over time (annual change noted).
- **Deviation**: The angle between magnetic north and compass north, caused by the vessel's own magnetic field. Found on the deviation card, which must be checked regularly.
- **Conversion**: True to compass — apply variation (westerly add, easterly subtract to get magnetic) then deviation. Mnemonic: "CADET" — Compass ADd East = True, or "Error West, Compass Best; Error East, Compass Least."

## Common Exam Questions

1. Talk me through how you would plan a passage from the English Channel to the Baltic Sea.
2. How would you calculate the UKC for a port entry at a secondary port?
3. Your GPS has failed. How do you continue to navigate safely?
4. Explain the difference between a radar range fix and a visual bearing fix in terms of accuracy.
5. How do you check the accuracy of your ECDIS position?
6. What are the limitations of relying solely on ECDIS for navigation?
7. Describe parallel indexing and how you would use it when transiting a channel.

## Key Points to Remember

- **Never rely on a single source of position information.** GPS must always be cross-checked.
- **UKC policy**: Most companies require a minimum of 10% of draught in open water, increasing in confined waters. Know your company policy.
- **Squat calculation**: Squat increases with speed squared. In shallow water, squat can be significant. Candidates should know at least one formula (e.g., Barrass: Squat = Cb x V^2 / 100 in open water).
- **ECDIS is not infallible**: Datum shifts, ENC update delays, display scale limitations, and over-reliance are all real risks.
- **Admiralty charts use LAT** (Lowest Astronomical Tide) as chart datum — depths shown are the minimum you can expect under normal conditions.

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners will probe the candidate's practical ability to plan and monitor a passage. They expect candidates to demonstrate a systematic approach to appraisal and planning, to articulate what information they would gather and from where, and to show awareness of the limitations of each navigational tool. A common weakness is over-reliance on GPS without understanding how to verify its accuracy or what to do when it fails. Examiners also frequently test tidal calculations, particularly for secondary ports, and expect candidates to show competence with both paper charts and ECDIS.

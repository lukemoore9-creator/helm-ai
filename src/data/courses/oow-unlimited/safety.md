# Safety — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

Safety at sea encompasses a broad range of topics that an OOW must master: emergency procedures, fire-fighting, life-saving appliances (LSA), personal survival, search and rescue, and the ISM Code. The MCA oral examination places significant emphasis on the candidate's practical understanding of how to respond to emergencies, their knowledge of onboard safety systems, and their awareness of the regulatory framework that governs safety management. An OOW who cannot articulate clear, decisive actions in an emergency will not pass.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **SOLAS Chapter II-2** — Fire protection, fire detection and fire extinction
- **SOLAS Chapter III** — Life-saving appliances and arrangements
- **SOLAS Regulation III/19** — Emergency training and drills
- **ISM Code** (International Safety Management Code) — SOLAS Chapter IX
- **IAMSAR Manual** (International Aeronautical and Maritime Search and Rescue), Volumes I-III
- **SOLAS Regulation V/33.1** — Distress messages: obligations and procedures
- **LSA Code** (International Life-Saving Appliance Code)
- **FSS Code** (International Fire Safety Systems Code)
- **MCA MSN 1823** — Musters, drills, and onboard training
- **STCW Code A-VI/1** — Basic safety training requirements

## Core Concepts

### Fire Safety and Fire-Fighting

Fire is one of the greatest dangers at sea. An OOW must understand:

- **The fire triangle**: Heat, fuel, oxygen. Remove any one element to extinguish the fire.
- **Classes of fire**: Class A (solid combustibles), Class B (flammable liquids), Class C (flammable gases), Class D (metals), Class F (cooking oils/fats). Electrical fires are not a separate class but require specific precautions.
- **Fixed fire-fighting systems**: CO2 (engine room, cargo holds), foam (machinery spaces, tank decks), water spray/sprinkler (accommodation, machinery), dry powder, inert gas systems (cargo tanks on tankers).
- **Portable extinguishers**: Water, foam, CO2, dry powder. The OOW must know which extinguisher suits which fire class.
- **Fire detection systems**: Smoke detectors (optical and ionisation), heat detectors (fixed temperature and rate-of-rise), flame detectors, manual call points.
- **Structural fire protection**: A-class and B-class divisions, fire zones, fire doors, fire dampers, ventilation shutdown procedures.
- **Engine room fire procedures**: Boundary cooling, evacuation of personnel, sealing the space, activation of fixed extinguishing system (CO2 release protocol — alarm, evacuation, confirmation of personnel clear, time delay, then release).

### Life-Saving Appliances

- **Lifeboats**: Totally enclosed, partially enclosed, free-fall. Capacity, launching procedures, on-load and off-load release mechanisms, common causes of accidents during drills.
- **Liferafts**: SOLAS A pack vs B pack, hydrostatic release units (HRU), manual launching, painter line operation, weak link, righting a capsized liferaft.
- **Rescue boats**: Fast rescue boats, MOB procedures, launching and recovery in a seaway.
- **Lifebuoys**: Minimum requirements (at least one on each side with self-igniting light, at least one with buoyant lifeline, at least two with self-activating smoke signals).
- **Lifejackets**: Donning requirements, lights, whistles, retroreflective tape.
- **EPIRB**: 406 MHz (with GPS), float-free and manual activation, registration, testing procedures.
- **SART**: Search and Rescue Transponder — 9 GHz, battery life (96 hours standby, 8 hours transmission), radar display pattern (12 dots becoming arcs).

### ISM Code (International Safety Management Code)

- **Purpose**: Establish a safety management system (SMS) to ensure safety at sea, prevent human injury and loss of life, and avoid damage to the marine environment.
- **Key elements**: Safety and environmental protection policy, Designated Person Ashore (DPA), master's responsibility and authority, shipboard operations procedures, emergency preparedness, reporting and analysis of non-conformities/accidents, maintenance of ship and equipment, documentation, company verification/review/evaluation.
- **Document of Compliance (DOC)**: Issued to the company.
- **Safety Management Certificate (SMC)**: Issued to the ship.
- **OOW role**: Understanding how the SMS applies to daily operations, reporting deficiencies and near-misses, participating in drills and audits.

### Search and Rescue

- **IAMSAR Vol III** (Mobile Facilities): The shipboard reference for SAR operations.
- **Initial actions on receiving a distress**: Alter course toward the distress position, increase lookout, prepare rescue equipment, inform Master, maintain listening watch.
- **SAR patterns**: Expanding square, sector search, track line (ahead and return), parallel sweep. The OOW should know when each is appropriate.
- **On-scene coordination**: The first vessel on scene may act as On-Scene Coordinator (OSC) until relieved by the RCC or another vessel.
- **MOB procedures**: Williamson Turn (delays in response), Anderson Turn (immediate response), Scharnow Turn (person lost from view for some time). Know the procedures, application, and limitations of each.

## Common Exam Questions

1. You discover a fire in the engine room. Walk me through your immediate actions as OOW.
2. Describe the procedure for releasing CO2 into the engine room.
3. What are the contents of a SOLAS A pack liferaft?
4. You receive a Mayday relay. What are your obligations and immediate actions?
5. Explain the role of the Designated Person Ashore under the ISM Code.
6. How would you conduct an expanding square search? When is it used?
7. A crew member falls overboard and is seen. What turn do you execute and what are the steps?

## Key Points to Remember

- **Fire boundary cooling** must begin immediately — do not wait for the fixed system to be activated.
- **CO2 release**: NEVER release until all personnel are confirmed evacuated from the space. The time delay alarm (typically 20 seconds for machinery spaces) exists for this reason.
- **HRU on liferafts**: Operates at approximately 4m depth, releases the raft which then floats free. The painter pulls to inflate, and the weak link breaks to separate from the sinking vessel.
- **Williamson Turn**: Immediate hard-over helm, shift helm when 60 degrees off original course, steady on reciprocal. Best when response is delayed.
- **ISM audits**: Internal audits annually, external audits (flag state or RO) at intermediate intervals.
- **Muster list**: Must be posted before the ship proceeds to sea. All crew must be familiar with their duties.

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners will present emergency scenarios and expect a calm, systematic response. They want to hear clear priorities: raise the alarm, muster crew, assess the situation, contain the emergency, and communicate. Candidates who jump to solutions without first ensuring the safety of personnel will be marked down. The ISM Code is increasingly examined — candidates must understand not just the theory but how it applies to their daily watchkeeping role. MOB procedures are almost guaranteed to come up, and the examiner will expect the candidate to articulate the correct turn, the reasoning behind it, and the recovery procedure.

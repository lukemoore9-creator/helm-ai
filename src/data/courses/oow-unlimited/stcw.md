# STCW (International Convention on Standards of Training, Certification and Watchkeeping for Seafarers) — OOW Unlimited

## Overview

The STCW Convention and Code set the global standards for the training, certification, and watchkeeping of seafarers. For the OOW Unlimited candidate, STCW defines the competencies they must demonstrate, the structure of their certification, and the principles governing their watchkeeping duties. The MCA examiner will expect candidates to understand not only their own certification requirements but also the broader framework of watchkeeping standards, hours of rest, fitness for duty, and the responsibilities of the Master and company in maintaining STCW compliance.

## Key Regulations / Standards

- **STCW Convention 1978** as amended (Manila Amendments 2010)
- **STCW Code Part A** — Mandatory standards
- **STCW Code Part B** — Recommended guidance
- **STCW Regulation II/1** — Mandatory minimum requirements for certification of officers in charge of a navigational watch on ships of 500 GT or more
- **STCW Code A-II/1** — Specification of minimum standard of competence for OOW
- **STCW Code A-VIII/1** — Fitness for duty
- **STCW Code A-VIII/2** — Watchkeeping arrangements and principles
- **MLC 2006** — Maritime Labour Convention (hours of work and rest)
- **MCA MSN 1877** — Hours of work and rest

## Core Concepts

### STCW Certification Structure

The STCW Convention establishes a tiered certification system for deck officers:

- **STCW II/1**: Officer in Charge of a Navigational Watch (OOW) — vessels of 500 GT or more
- **STCW II/2**: Master and Chief Mate — vessels of 3,000 GT or more
- **STCW II/3**: OOW and Chief Mate — vessels of less than 500 GT (near-coastal voyages)
- **STCW II/4**: Ratings forming part of a navigational watch
- **STCW II/5**: Ratings as able seafarer deck

For OOW Unlimited (STCW II/1), the candidate must demonstrate competence in:
- Navigation at the operational level
- Cargo handling and stowage at the operational level
- Controlling the operation of the ship and care for persons onboard at the operational level
- Radiocommunications at the operational level

### Competence Tables (STCW Code A-II/1)

The competence tables specify four columns for each function:
1. **Competence**: What the candidate must be able to do
2. **Knowledge, understanding and proficiency**: The underlying knowledge required
3. **Methods for demonstrating competence**: How competence is assessed (examination, approved training, in-service assessment)
4. **Criteria for evaluating competence**: The standard the candidate must meet

Key competence areas include: plan and conduct a passage, maintain a safe navigational watch, use of radar and ARPA, use of ECDIS, respond to emergencies, respond to a distress signal, use IMO Standard Marine Communication Phrases, manoeuvre the ship, and monitor loading/stability.

### Hours of Rest (STCW Code A-VIII/1)

This is a critically important practical topic. The regulations set minimum rest hours:

- **Minimum 10 hours of rest in any 24-hour period**, which may be divided into no more than two periods, one of which shall be at least 6 hours in length.
- **Minimum 77 hours of rest in any 7-day period.**
- Exceptions are permitted for emergencies, drills, and overriding operational conditions, but these must be documented and rest must be compensated as soon as practicable.
- A rest period may be reduced to a minimum of 6 consecutive hours provided such reductions do not extend beyond two days and at least 70 hours of rest are provided in the 7-day period.

**Records of hours of rest** must be maintained, available for inspection, and signed by both the seafarer and the Master. Non-compliance is a detainable deficiency under PSC.

### Watchkeeping Principles (STCW Code A-VIII/2)

Part 3 of Section A-VIII/2 sets out the principles to be observed in keeping a navigational watch:

- **The OOW is the Master's representative** and is at all times responsible for the safe navigation of the ship.
- The OOW shall not leave the bridge unattended at any time.
- The OOW shall not hand over the watch if there is reason to believe the relieving officer is unfit for duty (e.g., through fatigue or the effects of alcohol).
- **Before taking over the watch**, the relieving officer shall satisfy themselves as to: the ship's position, course, speed, draught, and UKC; any standing orders of the Master; the navigational situation (traffic, weather, visibility, hazards); the condition of navigational equipment; any outstanding tasks.
- **During the watch**, the OOW shall: maintain a proper lookout, check the course steered and position at regular intervals, monitor weather and sea conditions, use radar and other aids, maintain the bridge logbook, call the Master when in any doubt.
- **Calling the Master**: The OOW must call the Master whenever: visibility deteriorates, traffic conditions cause concern, difficulty maintaining course, failure to sight land or navigation mark at expected time, unexpected land or mark sighted, equipment failure, heavy weather, any other emergency or doubt.

### Fitness for Duty

- **Alcohol limits**: Blood alcohol level not exceeding 0.05% or 0.08% as determined by the flag state administration (UK uses 0.08% — but note the Merchant Shipping Act sets a lower limit of 0.05% for certain roles). In practice, many companies operate a zero-tolerance policy.
- **Drug and alcohol policies**: Companies are required to have policies under the ISM Code.
- **Fatigue management**: Recognised as a major contributor to maritime casualties. The OOW must be aware of their own fitness and must not take over the watch if impaired by fatigue.

### Manila Amendments 2010

Key changes introduced:
- Updated competence requirements including ECDIS training (mandatory)
- Enhanced training for service on tankers (oil, chemical, gas)
- Security-related training (ISPS Code awareness)
- Refresher and updating training requirements for all certificates
- Standards for medical fitness (STCW Regulation I/9)
- Electro-Technical Officer (ETO) certification introduced (STCW III/6)
- Updated rest hours provisions

## Common Exam Questions

1. What are the minimum hours of rest required under STCW and how should they be arranged?
2. What should you do before taking over the navigational watch?
3. Under what circumstances must you call the Master?
4. What are the competence requirements for an OOW under STCW II/1?
5. A crew member appears to be under the influence of alcohol when coming to relieve you. What do you do?
6. What are the responsibilities of the OOW as stated in STCW Code A-VIII/2?
7. What changes were introduced by the Manila Amendments?

## Key Points to Remember

- **10 hours rest in 24 hours, 77 hours in 7 days** — these numbers must be known precisely.
- **Never leave the bridge unattended** — this is a fundamental STCW requirement. If you need to leave (e.g., to investigate an alarm), another competent person must be on the bridge.
- **The OOW must not hand over the watch** if they believe the relieving officer is unfit. This includes fatigue, alcohol, illness, or any impairment. Inform the Master.
- **Calling the Master is never wrong** — STCW and good seamanship both require the OOW to call the Master early rather than late. The examiner will reward candidates who demonstrate a low threshold for calling the Master.
- **ECDIS training** is now mandatory for all OOW certificates issued after 2017.
- **Certificate revalidation**: Certificates are valid for 5 years. Revalidation requires evidence of continued competence (sea service, approved training, or approved test).

## Examiner Focus Areas

Examiners use STCW as a framework for discussing watchkeeping practices, professional standards, and the responsibilities of the OOW. They will test whether the candidate understands the practical application of hours of rest (not just the numbers but how to manage them in real operations), the procedure for taking over and handing over a watch, and the circumstances requiring the Master to be called. Candidates who can articulate the link between STCW requirements and safe navigation will perform well. The examiner may also test knowledge of the Manila Amendments, particularly the mandatory ECDIS training requirement and the updated tanker endorsement requirements.
